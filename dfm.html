DFM: Titanium Ring Shell Redesign for PVD Coating Yield

Mukund Vijay · Fictional scenario

Situation

A smart ring product line offered titanium shells in 10 finish variants across 12 sizes — 120+ SKU combinations. PVD coating yield was only 78% due to defects at sharp inner radii and channel grooves. Each rejected shell cost $18, creating $1.2M in annual scrap.

DFM Analysis & Changes

Defect Pareto

  • 48% — Coating thin spots at inner radii (film below 0.8μm target)
  • 28% — Adhesion failures at channel groove transition
  • 15% — Surface contamination from pre-coating cleaning residue
  • 9% — Color variation (batch-to-batch ΔE > 2.0)

CHANGE 1 — RADIUS INCREASE

Increased inner radii from R=0.15mm to R=0.4mm. FEA confirmed structural integrity (safety factor 4.2→3.8, still above 2.0 requirement). Coating simulation confirmed uniform film ≥1.0μm at all surfaces.

Change 2 — Channel Groove Redesign

Changed from vertical walls (90°) to tapered walls (75°), reduced depth from 0.6mm to 0.45mm. Maintained bonding surface area by widening groove opening. Adhesion pull test: >15N/mm².

Change 3 — Fixture Redesign

Redesigned PVD fixture to hold rings by flat inner surface (eliminated coating shadow). Added planetary rotation for uniform deposition. Fixture cost: $8K for 12-size set.

Change 4 — Cleaning Process

Replaced manual IPA wipe with automated ultrasonic cleaning in aqueous detergent + DI water rinse + nitrogen blow-dry. Eliminated operator-dependent residue variation.

Results

METRICBEFORE DFMAFTER DFM
PVD coating yield78%96.5%
Annual scrap cost$1.2M$180K
Tooling investment—$35K
ROI—29x ($1.02M / $35K)

Key Takeaways

The most impactful change (radius increase) cost nothing in tooling — it was a CAD revision that improved yield by 12 percentage points on its own. DFM reviews should happen before tooling commitment, when design changes are free.
