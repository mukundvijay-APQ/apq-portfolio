CAPA: Battery Swelling in a Fitness Wearable

Mukund Vijay · Fictional scenario

Situation

A consumer electronics company shipping 2M+ units annually of a fitness wearable began receiving elevated RMA returns citing "device bulging" and "band popping off." Customer support data showed a 3x increase in battery-related complaints over a 6-week window, concentrated in units produced at the primary CM in Shenzhen between weeks 38–44.

Initial triage classified returns under "cosmetic/enclosure," delaying escalation. When a quality engineer physically inspected 15 returned units, 12 showed visible lithium-polymer cell swelling — a potential safety concern requiring immediate structured response.

CAPA Framework Applied

1. Identification & Documentation

Opened formal CAPA record (CAPA-2025-047) with cross-functional team assignment: Quality Engineering (lead), Electrical Engineering, Supply Chain, and the CM quality team. Documented failure mode: LiPo cell swelling causing enclosure deformation, concentrated in specific production lot range.

2. Immediate Containment

  • Issued lot hold on 12,000 units from weeks 38–44 still in CM warehouse and distribution pipeline
  • Initiated targeted outreach to customers with affected serial numbers via app notification
  • Established expedited return pathway with prepaid shipping for affected units
  • CM halted use of suspect battery cell lot pending investigation

3. Root Cause Investigation

Cross-referenced RMA data against incoming inspection records, bill of materials changes, and supplier lot traceability.

ROOT CAUSE FINDING

Direct cause: Elevated moisture content in the electrolyte of LiPo cells from Supplier B's lot #LC-2025-0938, causing accelerated gas generation during charge/discharge cycling.

Contributing cause: Supplier B had changed their electrolyte drying process (reduced oven time from 48h to 36h) to increase throughput — without filing an engineering change notification (ECN).

Systemic cause: No incoming inspection protocol for electrolyte moisture content; supplier change notification process relied on voluntary disclosure rather than contractual requirement.

4. Corrective Action

  • Supplier B reverted to original 48-hour electrolyte drying process; provided 8D report with process revalidation data
  • Implemented incoming moisture testing (Karl Fischer titration) for all LiPo cell lots — acceptance criteria: ≤20 ppm
  • Added battery cell swelling to accelerated life testing (ALT) protocol: 500-cycle test at 45°C with dimensional measurement at 100-cycle intervals

5. Preventive Action

  • Updated supplier quality agreement (SQA) with mandatory change notification clause requiring 90-day advance notice
  • Added electrolyte moisture content to incoming inspection checklist for all battery suppliers
  • Integrated battery swelling risk into FMEA for all current and future products using LiPo chemistry
  • Established quarterly supplier audit cadence with process parameter verification

Key Takeaways

The initial misclassification of returns as "cosmetic" delayed the CAPA trigger by 3 weeks. This case led to implementing automated RMA classification that flags battery-adjacent failure modes for immediate quality engineering review. The systemic fix — contractual change notification requirements — was more impactful than the immediate corrective action.
