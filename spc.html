SPC: Catching LED Alignment Drift Before Yield Impact

Mukund Vijay · Fictional scenario

Situation

A wearable ring manufacturer ran PPG sensor assembly across two shifts at their primary CM in Estonia. The LED-to-photodiode alignment was the #1 CTQ parameter — a 50μm shift could degrade heart rate accuracy by 15%. Historical yield was 98.2%, but the quality team suspected drift between weekly manual Cpk reviews.

SPC Implementation

Control Chart Design

CTQ: X/Y placement offset of green LED relative to primary photodiode. Specification: ±30μm. Target: 0μm.

X-bar and R charts, subgroup n=5, sampled every 25 units. Baseline Cpk: X-axis 1.67, Y-axis 1.82.

Applied Western Electric rules for out-of-control detection.

SPC ALERT — WEEK 5, SHIFT B

Trigger: WE Rule 4 — 5 of 6 points beyond +1σ on X-axis LED placement

Cpk at alert: 1.41 (declining from 1.67 baseline)

Projected Cpk in 2 weeks without intervention: ~1.05

Estimated yield impact if uncaught: 98.2% → ~94% within 3 weeks

Investigation

Pick-and-place nozzle on Line 2 showed 8μm of wear on vacuum tip — causing slight X-axis bias. Nozzle in service for 120,000 cycles (rated for 100,000). Shift B ran Line 2 exclusively.

Corrective Action

  • Replaced nozzle; verified X-axis offset returned to +1.8μm (within baseline)
  • Established nozzle replacement schedule at 90,000 cycles (10% safety margin)
  • Added nozzle cycle count to SPC dashboard as leading indicator

Impact

METRICWITHOUT SPCWITH SPC
Time to detect drift~3 weeks3 days
Units produced during drift~45,000~6,000
Estimated yield loss$270K$18K
Customer-facing defectsLikelyNone

Key Takeaways

The SPC system caught a trend that would not have triggered a simple pass/fail inspection — every individual unit was still within specification. The power of SPC is detecting process shifts before they become defects. Adding nozzle cycle count as a leading indicator transformed the system from reactive to predictive.
